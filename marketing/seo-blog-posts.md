# My Car Won't Start in Maine Winter — What to Check First

It happens every January and February across Maine: you walk out to your car on a 5°F morning, turn the key, and... nothing. Or a slow, grinding crank. Or just a click. **Car won't start in a Maine winter?** Here are the most likely causes and what to do.

## Reason #1: Dead or Weak Battery

Cold weather is the #1 killer of car batteries. A battery that's "fine" in September can fail completely when temperatures drop below 10°F. If you hear a click or your lights are dim, it's almost certainly the battery.

**What to do:** Jump-start the car and get the battery tested immediately. A battery test at Up Front Auto Repair in Standish is free with any service. If it's under 400 cold cranking amps (CCA), it's time for a replacement before you get stranded on Route 25.

## Reason #2: Thick Engine Oil

In extreme cold, conventional motor oil thickens and doesn't flow properly. This makes the engine harder to turn over and can prevent starting.

**What to do:** If you're running conventional oil, consider switching to a 0W-20 or 5W-30 synthetic for Maine winters. Synthetic oils flow much better at low temperatures.

## Reason #3: Frozen Fuel Line or Fuel Issues

Less common in fuel-injected modern vehicles, but if you have a very old car or if moisture got into your fuel system, this can happen in extreme cold.

**What to do:** A fuel system additive (like HEET) can help. If problems persist, bring it in for diagnosis.

## Reason #4: Bad Starter or Alternator

If your battery tests fine but the car still won't turn over, the starter motor may be failing. If the car starts but the battery keeps dying, your alternator might not be charging it.

**What to do:** Come see us. We'll diagnose it accurately and tell you exactly what it needs — no guessing, no unnecessary parts.

## Prepare Before Winter Hits

The best strategy is prevention. Before Maine winter sets in, have your battery, antifreeze/coolant mix, belts, and oil checked. Up Front Auto Repair in Standish offers a free winter readiness inspection with any fall service.

# How to Find a Mechanic You Can Trust — A Guide for Car Owners Who've Been Burned Before

If you're reading this, you've probably had a bad experience with a mechanic. Maybe you were overcharged. Maybe work was done without your approval. Maybe you were told you needed $1,200 in repairs and got a second opinion somewhere else for $200. You're not alone — it's one of the most common consumer complaints in America.

Here's **how to find a mechanic you can trust** — and what to do once you've found one.

## Step 1: Look at Reviews Carefully

Google and Yelp reviews are your first filter, but read them critically. Look for:
- **Specificity** — "They fixed my alternator quickly and for the price quoted" is more trustworthy than "Great service!"
- **Responses to negative reviews** — How a shop handles complaints tells you a lot about their character
- **Patterns** — Multiple reviews mentioning surprise charges? That's a pattern.

## Step 2: Ask the Right Questions Before You Commit

When you call or drop in, ask:
- "Do you give written estimates before starting work?"
- "Do you call for approval before doing additional repairs?"
- "What's your diagnostic fee, and is it applied toward the repair?"

A trustworthy shop will answer these questions clearly and without hesitation. If they're evasive, walk away.

## Step 3: Start with a Small Job

Before you hand over your keys for a major repair, start with something minor — an oil change, a tire rotation, an inspection. See how they communicate. Do they explain what they found? Do they pressure you into extras? Is the final bill what they quoted?

## Step 4: Trust Your Gut

When you walk into a shop, pay attention. Is it clean and organized? Do the staff treat you with respect? Do they explain things clearly or talk over your head? A good mechanic respects their customers.
